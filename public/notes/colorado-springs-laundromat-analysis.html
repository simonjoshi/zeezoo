Why that $369k laundromat is overpriced.

April 2026 · 10 min read

Someone sent me a listing for a coin laundry in Colorado Springs. $369k asking price, $49k annual cash flow, unmanned operation, "prime corner location." Sounds like the boring, safe alternative to risky boba acquisitions, right?

I ran it through Conductor. The verdict is 🔴 FAIL.

Here's why the numbers don't work and what a real offer would look like.

The Math is Broken

The stated $49k SDE is mathematically impossible. Rent alone consumes 42% of gross revenue. After water, utilities, insurance, and labor, the real cash flow is $15-25k/year, not $49k.

Let me show the math:

Gross Revenue: $78,840
Less: Rent ($2,778/mo): -$33,336
= $45,504 BEFORE utilities

Less: Water/gas/electric (20-30% of revenue): -$15,765 to -$23,652
Less: Insurance, supplies, repairs: -$5,000 to -$8,000
Less: Labor (coin servicing, cleaning, 2 employees): -$15,000 to -$25,000

Real SDE estimate: $15-25k/year

The seller's $49k number only works if they're:

  • Excluding rent (using it as a personal "add-back")
  • Excluding water and utilities (impossible for a laundromat)
  • Including depreciation deductions as if they're cash (they're not)
  • Simply inflating the number for sale

Pick one. All of them are red flags.

The Smoking Gun: "Not SBA Eligible"

This phrase is buried in the listing. And it's the most important signal in the entire deal.

SBA loans are absolutely available for laundromats. So why is this deal "not SBA eligible"? Because the lender is requiring a "cash buyer." That's code for: SBA underwriters rejected this deal.

Here's the calculation SBA used:

Loan amount: $369,000
Interest rate: ~10.5%
Term: 10 years
Annual debt service: ~$59,000

SBA requirement: SDE must be ≥ 1.25x debt service
Required SDE: $59,000 × 1.25 = $73,750 minimum

Claimed SDE: $49,000
Shortfall: $24,750

Professional underwriters looked at this deal and concluded: the asking price is too high for the actual cash flow.

"Cash buyer only" isn't a feature. It's a workaround to bypass the only scrutiny that matters.

The Lease Risk (The Killer)

Current rent: $2,778/month = $33,336/year.

That's 42% of gross revenue. That's catastrophic.

Here's the problem: a coin laundromat can't move. The plumbing, electrical, water hookups, and coin machine infrastructure are all fixed to this location. The landlord knows you're trapped. At lease renewal in 2029 (3.5 years), they'll price you out.

What happens at renewal?

  • Hold flat (unlikely): Rent stays $33k/yr, SDE preserved at $49k
  • 10% increase (realistic): Rent becomes $37k/yr, SDE drops to $45k
  • 20% increase (likely): Rent becomes $40k/yr, SDE drops to $42k
  • Market repricing: Rent becomes $45-55k/yr, SDE goes negative

You have 3.5 years to either negotiate a long extension NOW or plan your exit. Most buyers don't realize this until it's too late.

The Equipment Time Bomb

The business was established in 2020. The equipment is 6 years old.

Commercial washer lifespan: 10-14 years
Commercial dryer lifespan: 14-20 years
Major service window: years 7-10 (you're entering it)

Full equipment replacement for a 1,600 SF laundromat: $150-250k. That's 3-5 years of cash flow, gone. And the listing says "FF&E valued at $300k" — but used coin machines auction for 20-40% of replacement cost. The real liquidatable value is probably $60-120k.

You're not buying $300k of assets. You're buying aging machines on borrowed time.

How to Actually Value This Deal

Scenario A: Seller's Numbers Are Real

  • Cash-on-cash return: 13.3% (looks good on paper)
  • Payback period: 7.5 years
  • 10-year IRR: 10-12%

But: You're betting $369k on numbers that SBA underwriters rejected. No independent verification.

Scenario B: Realistic SDE ($25k/year)

  • Cash-on-cash return: 6.8%
  • Payback period: 14.8 years
  • 10-year IRR: 2-4% (barely above T-bills)

This is what the math actually supports. And it's worse than the S&P 500 with 100% of the risk.

Scenario C: Lease Renewal Surprise (2029)

Rent jumps to $3,500/month. SDE drops from $25k to $16k.

  • Cash-on-cash return: 4.3%
  • You're locked in, can't move, can't renegotiate

You're underwater.

If You Actually Want to Pursue This

Walk away from $369k. Counter at $150-180k. That math works:

  • $180k at $25k realistic SDE = 7.2x multiple (acceptable)
  • Cash-on-cash return: 14% (real)
  • Gives you cushion for equipment and lease surprises

But demand three things first:

  1. 3 years of tax returns. Not what the broker says. Actual filed returns from the owner.
  2. Bank deposits for the past 24 months. Verify the $78,840 revenue claim.
  3. POS data or coin machine reports. Show me the actual daily revenue, not aggregated numbers.

If the seller won't produce these, that's your answer. They're hiding something.

Boba vs. Laundromat: The Comparison

The laundromat feels safe because it's boring. People always need to do laundry. But look at the risk profile:

  • Boba: Higher complexity, owner-dependent, trend risk, regulatory risk — but higher upside if you execute, and seller financing is actually negotiable
  • Laundromat: Lower complexity, unmanned, utility demand — but deal is overpriced, lease risk is hidden, equipment is aging, landlord will squeeze you at renewal

The boba shop at least has upside. This laundromat is a landlord's cash cow. You're the tenant.
