Note 002 · Funding

How to actually finance a food service acquisition.

April 2026 · 12 min read

When you're evaluating a $2.2M boba shop acquisition, the deal math depends entirely on how you finance it. And there's a lot of bad information out there.

I had Zeezoo's research agent pull comprehensive data on SBA loans, seller financing, conventional alternatives, and newer fintech options. Then we debate-tested each one against a real food service acquisition. Then we accuracy-checked every claim, including rates, SBA rule changes, and common marketing deceptions.

Here's what actually works.

The Default Play: SBA 7(a)

If you're buying a restaurant, boba shop, cafe, or any food service business under $5M, the SBA 7(a) loan is your starting point. Not because it's the only option, but because it's the option the lending ecosystem is optimized for. Lenders know it. SBA knows it. The process is codified.

Here's what you're actually getting:

  • Up to $5M. Way more than you need, but the guarantee covers large acquisitions.
  • 10-year term for acquisitions. No balloon. This is crucial for food service cash flow.
  • 5% minimum cash injection. And it has to be YOUR cash, not borrowed against your house or family.
  • Goodwill-financeable. A boba shop is ~85% brand, location, customer list (goodwill). SBA loans explicitly allow you to finance that.
  • Rates around 9.5–9.75% in April 2026. That's Prime (6.75%) + 2.75–3%. Variable rate, resets quarterly.
  • 45–90 days to funding. Faster if you use an SBA Preferred Lender (PLP) like Live Oak, Huntington, Newtek, or Celtic.

The Critical Rule Change You Need to Know

In July 2025, the SBA updated its Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for seller notes. This change is not yet widely understood, and it will affect how you negotiate the purchase.

Old rule (pre-2025): A seller note could standby (unpaid) for the first 2 years, then the seller gets paid along with the SBA loan.

New rule (July 2025 onwards): A seller note must be on full standby for the entire life of the SBA loan. That means if you get a 10-year SBA loan, the seller waits 10 years before getting their money. Or you refinance early.

This is why seller willingness to finance is now a stronger signal. A seller who agrees to this is confident in the business. A seller who refuses is either uncertain or calculating.

Step-by-Step: How SBA 7(a) Actually Works

  1. Pre-qual letter (1 week): Call 2–3 SBA Preferred Lenders. Tell them your personal financial statement and net worth. They'll give you a soft pre-qual. This costs nothing and tells you if there are red flags upfront.
  2. LOI with 60–90 day close window (2 weeks): Make an offer with a financing contingency. Your lender will want time to underwrite.
  3. Due diligence (4 weeks): Collect 3 years of tax returns, P&Ls, the lease, equipment list, POS data, and payroll records. This is where most boba deals die. Many shops have under-reported cash sales. Demand the seller produce bank deposits and credit card settlement reports to verify real Seller Discretionary Earnings (SDE).
  4. Valuation (2–3 weeks): If goodwill exceeds $250K (likely for a $2.2M deal), the SBA requires a third-party business valuation. Costs $2–4K and adds time.
  5. Underwriting (4–8 weeks): Your lender submits the package to the SBA (or uses delegated authority if they're a PLP). They'll come back with conditions, re-trades, or questions.
  6. Closing: The SBA guarantee fee (3.0–3.75% on loans >$700K) gets rolled into your loan amount, not paid upfront.

Total timeline: 60–90 days from LOI to funding. Preferred Lenders can shave 2–3 weeks.

Seller Financing: The Money You Can Actually Negotiate

Here's a fact: the seller's willingness to carry paper is itself a signal about the business. If a seller won't finance any part of the deal, that tells you something. Maybe they're confident. Maybe they're running.

Here's how to structure it:

The Recommended Stack

5% your cash + 5% seller note (full standby, counts toward SBA equity) + 90% SBA 7(a)

This means you're "buying with 5% cash" and the seller is holding the first 5% in escrow until the SBA loan is paid off.

For that structure to work, you ask the seller for a 15–20% total seller note in your LOI. Then negotiate:

  • 5% on full standby (no payments until SBA is done)
  • 10–15% with 5–7 year amortization at 6–7% interest, payable concurrently with your SBA loan

Why does the seller agree to this? Because:

  • You're offering a higher purchase price in exchange (maybe 2–3% bump)
  • An earn-out tied to revenue retention (if you hit targets, the note gets paid faster)
  • A senior note structure (seller gets paid before other stakeholders, signaling confidence)

What Sellers Hate About the New Rule

They wait 10 years. Full stop. Some sellers will push back hard. Your leverage: offer a higher price, or an earn-out, or a 5-year refinance clause where you promise to refinance the SBA loan early if the business hits targets.

Pro tip: A seller who negotiates hard on this and then agrees? That's a signal they actually believe in the business.

Other Loan Types: When to Use Them

SBA Express

36-hour preliminary approval. Sounds fast, but there's a catch: only 50% SBA guarantee vs 75–85% for standard 7(a). Lenders take more risk, so they want 15–20% down instead of 5%. In practice, Express is slower and more expensive than standard 7(a), unless your deal is under $500K and you have a banking relationship that wants to move fast.

For your $2.2M boba shop? Skip it.

SBA 504

50–75% financing for real estate and equipment, at a fixed rate around 5.6–5.78%. Sounds cheaper than 7(a), right?

Catch: 504 cannot finance goodwill. It's real estate and equipment only. If you're buying a leasehold boba shop for $2.2M, that's almost entirely goodwill. 504 doesn't work here.

If you were buying the building too, then 504 might make sense for the real estate piece. But as-is, pass.

Conventional Bank Loans

Traditional banks will lend on food service, but they want 25–30% down and the rate is 8–12%. Shorter terms (5–7 years) with potential balloons. Unless you have significant liquid assets, SBA is better.

Equipment Financing

After close, if the espresso machine or blenders need replacing, you can get a separate equipment loan. 24–72 hour approval, rates 6–18% depending on lender and your credit. Use this post-acquisition for upgrades, not as your primary funding.

Business Lines of Credit (Bank)

Bank LOCs run 9.25%+, but they're revolving and unsecured (or lightly secured). Apply 6 months after close when you have 6 months of operating history. Don't use this for acquisition — use it for working capital cushion during slow seasons.

Fintech "Fast" Loans (Bluevine, Fundbox, OnDeck)

"Bluevine starts at 4.8% APR" / "Fundbox at 4.66%" — Every fintech loan marketing page

These are lies. Those are weekly rates, not APRs. The effective APR is 25–50%. I'm not exaggerating. The math:

  • Bluevine quotes "4.8% weekly" → that's ~250% APR
  • You repay in 6–12 months, so your effective rate is high and front-loaded
  • Use these only in emergencies, for 30-day working capital gaps, never for acquisition funding

Merchant Cash Advances (MCA) and Factoring

Effective APR 40–100%+. MCA repayment is tied to daily credit card sales, so when business is slow, you're still paying. Avoid entirely for acquisition funding. These are for survival, not growth.

ROBS (401k Rollover)

You roll your 401k into a C-corp ROBS entity, and use the cash for down payment. It's "free money" (no debt), but the risk is 100% of your retirement. The IRS compliance data shows most ROBS businesses fail or are heading toward failure. Treat this as: you're putting your retirement at risk to avoid debt. Only viable if you've already mentally prepared to lose the 401k.

Your Next Three Moves

Based on the $2.2M boba shop acquisition:

  1. Call 2–3 SBA Preferred Lenders this week. Live Oak, Huntington, Newtek, Celtic. Soft pre-qual based on your personal financial statement. No commitment, just intel. Ask about their typical SDE requirements for food service deals.
  2. When you get an LOI signed, negotiate 15–20% seller financing. Structure it: 5% standby + 10–15% concurrent. Reference the July 2025 SBA SOP change so the seller knows you're educated. This signals confidence.
  3. In due diligence, demand bank deposits and POS data. Many boba shops underreport cash sales. You need to verify the real SDE before committing to the debt load. This is non-negotiable.
