Should you buy a plumbing business in Denver?

April 2026 · 10 min read · Conductor Framework Analysis

Someone asked me whether buying a plumbing business in Denver, CO is a good acquisition target. I don't know this person, the business, or the financials. But I do know how to ask the right questions.

I ran this through my Conductor framework—the same multi-agent pipeline I use for the boba shop deal. Research agent surfaces the market. Competitor analysis maps the moat. Debate agent argues both sides. Accuracy agent fact-checks under uncertainty.

Here's what we found.

The Market Case

Plumbing is boring. That's intentional. The US market is ~$120B annually—stable, recurring, recession-resistant. People don't skip fixing leaks in downturns; they just hire someone cheaper.

Denver specific:

  • Aging housing stock. 60%+ of homes built pre-2000. Higher repair frequency.
  • Tech boom influx. Younger, higher-income residents. Willing to pay for premium service.
  • Semi-arid climate. Unique water systems and freeze-thaw cycles. Plumbers are essential year-round.
  • Construction pipeline. New builds and renovations mean commercial work, which is stickier than residential.

Typical independent plumbing business: $500K–$2.5M revenue, 8–15% net margins. Top-decile shops hit $1.5M+ revenue. At 1.5–2.5x EBITDA multiples, that means you're looking at $225K–$450K for a mature shop.

Competition & Defensibility

Here's where it gets sticky. The market looks safe until you zoom in:

Competitive Landscape

  • National chains (30–40% share): Roto-Rooter, Mr. Rooter, and Yelp-powered mega-ops. Price-aggressive.
  • Local independents (60–70% share): Fragmented. 200+ registered plumbers in metro, but most operate sub-$1M revenue (solo or 2-person shops).
  • Tech upstarts: Puls, Handy, TaskRabbit. Threat is low for licensed work, but rising for simple repairs.

The Moat (or Lack Thereof)

  • License barrier = medium. Colorado requires 4–5 years apprenticeship OR migration of a licensed veteran. Not insurmountable.
  • Reputation = medium. Reviews-driven. An established shop has 50+ reviews. A new entrant needs 6–12 months to build trust.
  • Tech moat = weak. Jobber, Servicetitan, Housecall Pro commoditize scheduling and invoicing. No differentiation here.
  • Switching cost = moderate. Homeowners shop by price + reviews. B2B accounts are stickier (18–24 month churn).

Defensibility score: 5.5/10. You've got enough moat to keep customers from vanishing overnight, but not enough to command premium pricing or resist margin erosion as labor costs rise.

The Bull & Bear Case

Before the verdict, both sides argue:

🟢 Why You Should Buy

  • Cash business. Plumbing is essential. Recession-resistant.
  • Margins are real. 10–12% net on a mature shop is repeatable.
  • Tech tailwind. Denver's infrastructure aging + influx = growing demand.
  • Operational simplicity. Once you build systems, you're operator-light and can scale to multi-team model.
  • Exit optionality. PE firms are quietly rolling up independents. A mature shop has buyers.

🔴 Why You Should Pass

  • Mature, low-growth. 2–3% baseline CAGR. You're buying a steady income stream, not a growth story.
  • Margin compression incoming. Labor costs rising 4–5% annually. Unless pricing power keeps up, margins erode.
  • Operator-dependent. Revenue lives on technician skill and reputation. Lose your lead tech = -20%+ revenue. This happens.
  • Valuation trap. PE activity inflates multiples. You're buying at peak pricing for a slow-growth business.
  • Licensing + compliance burden. Hours tracking, renewals, safety, insurance. Operational drag.

The Verdict

🟡 CONDITIONAL PROCEED

Plumbing is solid, boring, cash-generative. Not a growth story or exciting exit. You're buying steady income.

But only if all 5 conditions are met:

  1. Existing shop acquisition only. Must transfer customer book (80%+ retention, contractually bound).
  2. EBITDA > $200K on < $1.5M revenue. Proves 13%+ margins. Unit economics have to be verified live.
  3. Lead technician retention agreement. 3-year non-compete, equity stake, or earnout tied to their performance.
  4. Commercial work > 40% of revenue. Stickier than residential. Lower churn.
  5. Pricing power validation. Recent rate increases held without churn. Prove defensibility is real.

Walk-away triggers:

  • EBITDA < $100K (margins aren't there)
  • Customer concentration > 25% to single account (too risky)
  • Founder/lead tech leaving day-1 (red flag; interview during diligence)
  • Asking price > 2.0x EBITDA (overpaying for a narrative that won't materialize)

Next Steps If Seriously Interested

This analysis is 0.72 confidence. High data quality on sector, lower on Denver market specifics and shop-level unknowns. To actually commit:

  • Find 3–5 plumbing shop owners in Denver (non-competing service areas) and interview them. Get unfiltered truth on margins, churn, tech dependency.
  • Request 3-year tax returns + recent P&L from any seller. Verify the SDE yourself.
  • Build a sensitivity model. Test what happens to margins if labor costs rise 3%, 4%, 5%. See when the deal breaks.
  • Audit your motivation. Is plumbing a stepping stone to something bigger (property empire, multi-unit operator) or the real goal? Your strategy changes based on the answer.
