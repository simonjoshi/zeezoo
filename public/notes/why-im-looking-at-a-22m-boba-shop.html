Why I'm looking at a $2.2M boba shop.

April 2026 · 6 min read

I'm Simon. About a week ago I started seriously evaluating a small business acquisition — a boba tea and sandwich cafe in Arvada, Colorado, listed at $2.2M in trailing twelve-month revenue.

I'm not doing this alone. I've been working with an AI sidekick I named Zeezoo — a multi-agent system I built to help me think through complex decisions. Research agent. Debate agent. Accuracy agent. They argue with each other. They argue with me. It's a weird, useful workflow.

This is the first in a series. I'm going to publish what we find — the questions, the red flags, the math, the second-guessing — as we go. If I buy it, you'll see why. If I walk, you'll see why.

Why a boba shop?

Conventional acquisition wisdom says buy a laundromat or a car wash. Boring, recurring, recession-resistant. I started there. Then this listing came across my desk and three things made me pause:

  • Brand velocity. Boba is one of the few food categories still growing in the US (~13% CAGR through 2030, depending on the source).
  • Revenue mix. Drinks + sandwiches + claw machines. Three legs of a stool. Diversification at the SKU level.
  • Owner exit timing. Two years in. That's either "I'm tired" (workable) or "I'm running" (deal killer). Worth investigating.

Zeezoo, for the record, disagreed with me on this. He thinks laundromats print money and boba is a fashion trade. I asked him to argue both sides. He did. I'm still not convinced he's right.

"Boba is consumer discretionary with thin margins and trend risk. Laundromats are utilities with sticky local demand. You're choosing the harder business because it's more interesting. That's a bias, not an analysis." — Zeezoo (Debate Agent)

Fair. We'll see who's right.

What we found in week one.

I ran the listing through Zeezoo's full pipeline: Research → Debate → Accuracy verification. The output was about 8,000 words and 5 critical risk flags. Here are the top three:

  • Owner-dependent revenue. The current owner works the counter. Customers know him by name. Transition risk is real and underpriced in the asking price.
  • Claw machines = regulatory landmine. A meaningful chunk of revenue comes from claw machines. Colorado gambling regulators have pending guidance on prize machines. One ruling could vaporize 20–30% of revenue overnight.
  • Lease expires March 2029. That's three years and three rent negotiations away. The landlord can functionally take 20%+ of the value of the business at renewal.

None of these are dealbreakers. All of them are negotiation levers. We'll get into each one in upcoming notes.

What's next.

I sent the seller a list of financial questions — SDE, P&L, revenue breakdown by category, owner hours. Standard due diligence stuff. He's had two days to respond. He hasn't.

Zeezoo's take: "Sus. Either he's gathering real numbers or he's allergic to scrutiny. Wait 48 more hours, then pressure-test."

I'll let you know what comes back. Or doesn't.
