Deal Hunter

A Pac-Man game for business acquisition · April 2026

I built a game where the mechanic is finding and acquiring small businesses. You move around a grid, land on business opportunities, and decide whether to invest based on valuation, ROI, and risk. The goal is to build a portfolio of profitable businesses.

The Core Loop

Movement is pure Pac-Man—WASD or arrow keys across a 20×15 grid. Businesses spawn randomly with stats:

  • Valuation: The cost to acquire (ranges $50K–$2M)
  • Annual ROI: The return percentage (scaled by risk)
  • Risk Level: Low (green), medium (amber), or high (red)

You start with $1M capital. Step onto a business, a card pops up showing the deal, and you click "Acquire" if you can afford it. Each business you own generates passive annual income—that's your multiplier for buying more businesses.

Why This Matters

Most business acquisition advice is static: spreadsheets, case studies, analysis frameworks. But I wanted something interactive and playable—a tool that makes the feel of portfolio building visceral. How does capital constrain you? When do high-risk deals make sense? What does compounding your income actually feel like?

Deal Hunter answers those by putting you in the game. You experience the tradeoff between buying expensive, stable businesses vs. riskier, cheaper ones. You feel the friction of capital limits and the rush of generating enough income to unlock the next tier of deals.

Technical Approach

Built with React + Canvas for smooth grid rendering. The game state tracks:

  • Player position and movement
  • Business spawn logic (no overlaps)
  • Portfolio capital and owned businesses
  • Real-time net worth and annual income calculations

The UI makes the money flow obvious: show annual income per business, compare cost vs. your current cash, and highlight how much more you need if you can't afford a deal yet.

What's Next

The MVP is playable. Future iterations could add:

  • Market conditions that affect business valuations
  • Time mechanics (quarterly or annual income payouts)
  • Competitors trying to steal deals
  • Debt/leverage to buy more aggressively
  • Portfolio milestones and end-game conditions

But for now, the core loop works: hunt, acquire, earn, repeat. The game is ready to playtest and iterate on.
